Mango Slicer questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A mango slicer is a handheld gadget that presses a curved blade through the mango to separate the flesh from the pit in one motion, saving time and reducing stickiness. It works best on just-ripe mangoes and is a worthwhile convenience if you prepare them often, though it won’t replace a knife for precise dicing. Most well-reviewed models come from brands like OXO, Chef’n, Norpro, and Williams Sonoma, each offering a different balance of comfort and price.

What is a mango slicer and how does it work?

A mango slicer is a simple tool, usually a plastic or metal frame fitted with a sharp stainless steel blade shaped to follow the mango’s flat, oval pit. You cut off a small slice from one end of the mango to create a stable base, then press the slicer down through the fruit. The blade cuts two “cheeks” of flesh away from the pit in one smooth pass. Most designs are either a two-piece set (one slicer and one pitter) or an all-in-one tool that also scoops out the pit. The handle is often padded or textured for grip, since mango skins can be slippery. No batteries or electricity, just hand pressure.

Are mango slicers better than using a knife?

It depends on your priorities. A mango slicer is safer for casual cooks because the blade is partially guarded, and it delivers consistent, even slices without the risk of cutting toward your hand. It’s also faster for making snack-ready wedges or salad strips. However, a chef’s knife gives you far more control. With a knife you can dice the mango into cubes, trim away every bit of flesh around the pit, and handle odd-sized or very ripe fruit that a slicer might crush. A slicer also leaves a small amount of edible flesh on the pit that you’d otherwise cut off. For frequent mango prep, a slicer is a handy shortcut; for precision, reach for a knife.

What should I look for when buying a mango slicer?

Start with blade material, stainless steel is standard, but some blades are thicker and less likely to bend. Look for a blade that is sharp out of the box and stays sharp after repeated use. The handle matters, too: soft rubber or textured plastic gives a secure grip, especially when your hands are covered in mango juice. Consider dishwasher safety if you prefer easy cleanup, but be aware that the blade can dull faster in the dishwasher. Also check user reviews to see if the slicer works well with large mango varieties (some cheaper models are too small for big Ataulfo or Kent mangoes). Finally, a model that includes a separate pitter can be helpful for removing the center seed cleanly.

Can a mango slicer handle unripe or very ripe mangoes?

A mango slicer works best on fruit that is just ripe: slightly soft when squeezed but still firm enough to hold its shape. Unripe mangoes are too hard, the blade may struggle to push through, and you risk cracking the plastic frame or bending the blade. Very ripe, mushy mangoes tend to get crushed by the pressure, leaving a messy pulp rather than neat slices. If you often deal with very firm or very soft mangoes, a knife is more reliable. For the sweet spot of ripe-but-firm fruit, a slicer shines.

How do you clean and store a mango slicer?

Most mango slicers are dishwasher-safe, but hand washing with warm soapy water and a soft brush is gentler on the blade and the handle. Pay special attention to the crevice where the blade meets the frame, mango fibers get trapped there. Dry immediately to prevent any metal components from spotting. Store the slicer in a drawer with a blade guard if it came with one, or place it separately so it doesn’t nick other utensils. A few brands sell a protective cover or sheath; if yours didn’t include one, a simple paperboard sleeve works.

What are some trusted mango slicer brands?

OXO’s version has a soft, non-slip handle and a sharp stainless blade, consistently recommended for comfort and durability. Chef’n offers a colorful plastic frame with a good blade and often includes a separate pitter; it’s a popular mid-range choice. Norpro makes a simple, no-frills slicer at a budget-friendly price; it gets the job done but the handle may be less comfortable for heavy use. Williams Sonoma’s model features a full stainless steel construction and ergonomic grips, premium, but built to last. No matter which brand you pick, read recent owner reviews to confirm that the blade holds up and the slicer fits the size of mangoes you usually buy.

Frequently asked questions

Do mango slicers work on all mango varieties?

They work best on common varieties with a flat, oval pit, like Ataulfo, Tommy Atkins, or Kent. Fibrous or very flat mangoes (such as some Indian varieties) can jam the blade or leave ragged edges.

Are mango slicers safe for kids to use?

The blade is sharp, so adult supervision is essential. Some models have a safety lock or a handle that keeps fingers away from the blade. A few brands market their slicer as kid-friendly for supervised use.

Can I use a mango slicer for other fruits?

It’s designed specifically for mangoes. You might get a similar effect on a large peach or a nectarine with a flat pit, but it won’t work well on round-pit fruits like plums or avocados.

How many slices does a mango slicer make at once?

One pass usually produces two large oval pieces (the cheeks) plus the pit in the center. To get strips or cubes, you’d make crosswise cuts on each cheek with a knife afterward.

Do mango slicers come with a pitter?

Many sets include a separate pitter, a smaller tool that presses through the center to remove the seed. Some all-in-one models have a built-in pitting mechanism, but these can be less effective.

Is it worth spending more on a mango slicer?

In general, more expensive models use thicker stainless steel, more comfortable handles, and tighter assembly. A budget slicer may bend or dull quickly; a well-built one can last years if cared for. If you slice mangoes only occasionally, a mid-priced model is sufficient.

In shortA mango slicer is a small convenience that pays off if you prep mangoes regularly. Stick with a well-reviewed model from a reputable kitchen brand, and you’ll get neat slices with less stickiness. Just remember it’s a specialized tool, not a replacement for a chef’s knife when you need precise dices.
